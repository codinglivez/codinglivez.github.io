Internet of Things (IoT) Security: Key Challenges

By Neha Madhavi| 23 May | 3 Comments | IoT

According to a report by Statista, the number of connected devices comprising IoT across geographies currently stand at over 23 billion. By 2020, the number will be 30 billion, and by 2025, the world will see over 60 billion IoT-connected devices.Designing and managing robust and scalable IoT security solutions is dounting ad it requires deep security expertise in an impossible broad set of domains like hardware, software, networking, protocols, storage, etc. Across disciplines like design, manufacturing and operations practices. The black hats are aware of these deficiencies.

Fortunately, the folks at Open Web Application Security Project (OWASP) have taken the initiative a build a comprehensive list of IoT vulnerabilities. It correctly indicates that most of the threats are relatively easy to exploit. The reason behind this is that a botnet of weaponized IoT devices can have a disproportionately high impact on both consumers and enterprises. Dissecting IoT security for root causes results in some key challenges that need to be addressed.

Key Challenges :

  1. Resource-constrained Devices

    Portable devices are typically battery-powered but energy-consuming with very limited computational strength. The compute workload of certificate-based encryption and decryption is usually impractical. This restricts cyber security to a subset of IoT devices like manufacturing robots, vehicles, and medical machines. Thus, portable devices provide enough toeholds for black-hats in a world ever increasing smart portable devices.

  2. Organizational Silos

    IoT solutions include a full stack of components like sensors, hardware, firmware, networking, infrastructure, integration with enterprise systems that need to work together for robust, scalable end-to-end security. Since these components are owned by different divisions within multiple organizational silos.

  3. Inadequate Standards

    Standards typically address parts of an end-to-end IoT solutions and allow for the flexibility required in its domain. Else, all products complying with a standard would be identical but in reality, they are not designed to interoperate seamlessly with other standards. Many standards organizations, including the World Economic Forum, are working diligently on this issue. They are encouraging inclusion and collaboration of governments to contribute, adopt, and incentive adoption.
